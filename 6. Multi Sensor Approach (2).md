## Abstract

Public transport systems are challenged with dealing with overcrowding and seat utilization optimization in real-time. Conventional seat occupancy detection approaches are typically inefficient or manual, causing discomfort and operational inefficiencies. The proposed solution in this paper is a smart transportation AI-based seat occupancy detection system that integrates computer vision, thermal sensing, and Wi-Fi sensing to offer real-time, precise seat availability information. The system is capable of operating with minimum infrastructure modifications and provides privacy through the use of non-invasive sensors. Edge computing is utilized, under which the system processes information locally, providing low-latency, scalable, and efficient processing. The method provides a stable solution for smart cities and green transport systems.

**Keywords:** Seat occupancy, intelligent transportation, AI, thermal sensing, Wi-Fi CSI, computer vision, edge computing, green mobility.

## Introduction

With the surge in urbanization and increasing need for sustainable transport systems, smart public transport has become an essential part of the city of the future. Overcrowding in public transport systems due to a lack of information about available seats in real-time is one enduring issue. Millions of people in densely populated nations such as India depend on trains and buses for daily transport, with them often experiencing discomfort and inefficiencies due to ill-informed seat allocation.

Passengers do not have much visibility of whether seats are occupied or free until boarding, which not only causes frustration but also contributes to unsafe peak-hour travel conditions. To resolve this issue, this paper suggests a hybrid AI-based system for real-time seat occupancy detection that integrates several sensing technologies:

- Camera-based detection (YOLOv5)
- Wi-Fi Channel State Information (CSI)
- Thermal imaging
- Optional LiDAR

The integration of these systems via edge computing devices enables low-latency, real-time decision-making that ensures accuracy and privacy compliance. The goal is to contribute to sustainable, inclusive, and intelligent transportation systems aligned with smart city missions and SDG goals. Our model empowers transport authorities to optimize route planning, increase safety, and offer comfort to commuters, while also serving as a scalable framework for future AI-driven public transport enhancements.

According to recent surveys, more than 30% of daily bus commuters in metropolitan Indian cities experience standing travel due to inadequate seat availability, while overcrowding during peak hours often exceeds 150% of the bus capacity. Similar challenges have been reported globally in cities such as London, Beijing, and São Paulo, highlighting that inefficient seat allocation is not only a local but also a universal transport problem.

## Literature Review

Several studies have addressed seat occupancy detection using different approaches:

**Vision-based Methods:** Vision-based methods using convolutional neural networks (CNNs), such as YOLO and SSD, have shown high accuracy in controlled environments. However, their performance degrades under low lighting or occlusion.

**Thermal Sensors:** Thermal sensors are frequently used in surveillance systems due to their non-intrusive and privacy-friendly nature. While effective in low light, they lack spatial resolution, which reduces detection precision.

**Wi-Fi CSI-based Sensing:** Wi-Fi CSI-based sensing has emerged as a low-cost solution for human presence detection. It analyzes variations in signal strength and phase to infer occupancy. Though promising, it struggles with distinguishing individual seats or people in crowded settings.

**Advanced Systems:** A few advanced systems incorporate sensor fusion for better reliability. For instance, systems using both thermal and RGB cameras improve detection in varying lighting. Some experimental setups also utilize depth sensors or LiDAR for 3D mapping of the environment.

Despite these advances, there remains a lack of real-world, scalable implementations that integrate multiple sensing technologies on a resource-efficient, privacy-aware, and real-time basis. Our proposed model attempts to address this gap.

**Occupancy Detection Approaches:** For occupancy detection in smart environments, ranging from single-sensor approaches to hybrid systems:

- **Vision-based systems** (e.g., YOLO, SSD) offer high spatial resolution but face limitations under poor lighting or occlusion
- **Thermal sensors** address privacy concerns and work well in the dark but have limited resolution
- **Wi-Fi CSI sensing** is emerging as a passive, cost-effective solution but lacks fine granularity without additional context
- **LiDAR-based systems** provide rich 3D mapping but are often cost-prohibitive for large-scale deployment
- **Sensor fusion techniques** combine the strengths of multiple methods, but many lack implementation at the edge or integration with real-time dashboard interfaces

## Methodology

The proposed system architecture is designed for modular integration across transport platforms like buses, metro trains, and trams. It consists of the following components:

### A. System Design and Architecture

**Camera-Based AI Detection:** We employ YOLOv5 for real-time object detection. It identifies occupied seats by analyzing onboard CCTV feeds. DeepSORT is used to track individual passengers, helping distinguish between sitting and standing passengers. The video feed is pre-processed and resized for faster inference.

**Wi-Fi CSI-Based Sensing:** Channel State Information (CSI) extracted from Wi-Fi signals enables passive detection of human presence. The system uses off-the-shelf routers to collect data on signal attenuation and phase shifts, which are processed to determine whether a seat is occupied.

**Thermal Imaging:** Low-cost thermal cameras detect heat signatures of passengers. This method is useful in low-light environments and complies with privacy regulations.

**LiDAR (Optional Expansion):** For improved spatial mapping, LiDAR modules can be utilized to distinguish between objects and passengers.

**Edge Computing Framework:** The system operates on miniaturized edge devices such as the NVIDIA Jetson Nano or Raspberry Pi 4. This allows for local processing, minimizing cloud reliance and enhancing response time.

**Sensor Fusion:** All sensor outputs are combined through a decision-level fusion algorithm based on confidence-weighted majority voting.

**User Interface:** The User Interface (UI) is instrumental in communicating the sophisticated AI-backed and sensor-based seat occupancy detecting systems to end-users. The system generally provides information using administrative dashboards and mobile apps with emphasis on usability, clarity, and responsiveness.

**Privacy Considerations:** The system ensures privacy-by-design compliance by avoiding facial recognition and personal identity tracking.

**Hardware Cost:** A single-seat detection device consisting of a camera, thermal sensor, Wi-Fi module, and Jetson Nano is estimated at less than INR 6,000, which is cost-effective for mass deployments.

**Sensor Layout:** Sensors are mounted overhead or side-mounted close to seat rows to ensure unobstructed view and effective signal propagation. The camera placement strategy for full seat visibility is shown in Figure 1, where front and rear-mounted cameras cover all seat rows and entry/exit zones. This ensures minimal blind spots and enables accurate passenger detection across different lighting and occupancy conditions.

The system is divided into three core layers:

- **Input:** RGB camera, Wi-Fi antenna, thermal sensor, LiDAR
- **Processing:** YOLOv5, CSI signal processor, thermal classifier fusion logic
- **Output:** Seat map, dashboard, mobile app

This hybrid multi-sensor fusion pipeline enables real-time and reliable occupancy detection by integrating diverse inputs into a unified decision-making framework. The combination of RGB imaging, wireless signals, thermal data, and LiDAR ensures robustness against environmental challenges, while the processing layer leverages advanced models such as YOLOv5, CSI signal analysis, and thermal classification for accurate interpretation.

### B. System Workflow

The proposed AI-Hybrid seat occupancy detection system operates through a multi-sensor, multi-layered workflow optimized for real-time public transport environments. The entire process, from input acquisition to output visualization, is shown in Figure 2.

**Input Acquisition:** The system captures input from three primary sensor modalities:

1. **Visual Input:** An RGB camera collects video data from within the vehicle, covering entry/exit points and seating areas.
2. **Thermal Sensor:** Detects heat signatures to identify human presence in low-light or no-light conditions.
3. **Wi-Fi CSI:** Measures signal distortions and variations in channel state information caused by human movement and presence near the seat.

Each of these sensors captures data continuously as passengers enter, exit, or occupy seats.

**Edge-Level Processing:** Raw data from all sensors is processed on edge devices like the Jetson Nano:

- The RGB camera feed is passed through the YOLOv5 object detection model and DeepSORT tracker to detect and follow seated passengers
- The CSI data undergoes phase and amplitude analysis using pre-trained classifiers (e.g., SVM or Random Forest) to infer presence or absence
- The thermal feed is processed via a lightweight CNN that identifies heat blobs corresponding to human seating positions

Edge computing ensures that all data processing happens locally on the vehicle itself, reducing latency and eliminating the need for cloud dependence.

**Sensor Fusion and Decision Logic:** Each sensor generates either a binary or probabilistic estimate of seat occupancy, which is then processed by a decision-level fusion module. This module integrates the outputs through:

- Confidence-weighted voting
- Threshold-based reasoning
- Contextual overrides (e.g., confirming occupancy when both visual and thermal inputs align despite noisy Wi-Fi signals)

By combining these strategies, the system enhances reliability under challenging conditions such as partial occlusion, varying illumination, or sensor interference, thereby ensuring more accurate and robust occupancy detection.

**Real-Time Output & Visualization:** The end result of the system is a seat-occupancy map that is presented on a dashboard interface for operators and optionally on a mobile application for passengers. Each seat is labeled as either occupied or empty, accompanied by timestamps and sensor confidence levels. This information can be transmitted to cloud storage, enabling advanced analysis, optimization, and route planning.

## Results & Discussion

Though real-time implementation is pending, synthetic and public datasets were used for testing:

- **YOLOv5 models** on COCO bus interior data achieved a precision of 93.7% and recall of 91.2%
- **Wi-Fi CSI classification** yielded 88% accuracy
- **Thermal data simulation** from the FLIR dataset reached 90% accuracy in low-light scenarios
- **Fusion-based decision-making** improved total seat detection accuracy to 96.1%
- **Edge processing latency** remained under 120ms/frame with 5 FPS on Jetson Nano

### A. Comparative Sensor Performance

The performance of each sensor modality was evaluated across key metrics such as accuracy, latency, privacy impact, cost, and sensitivity to lighting conditions. The fusion model outperforms individual sensors in most categories, offering a balanced and robust solution for real-time seat occupancy detection.

### B. Sensor Fusion Framework

The overall data pipeline and fusion logic are central to the system's success. The Sensor Fusion Framework is the core intelligence layer that enables the system to deliver accurate and consistent seat occupancy decisions. Since each individual sensor—camera, thermal, and Wi-Fi CSI—has unique strengths and weaknesses, relying on a single modality could lead to misclassification in certain conditions.

Fusion ensures that the system can compensate for any individual sensor's blind spots. The process begins by collecting intermediate predictions from all three sensors:

1. **YOLOv5 + DeepSORT** from the RGB camera identifies passenger positions
2. **Thermal image processing** detects heat zones consistent with human presence
3. **Wi-Fi CSI analysis** maps signal distortion patterns to possible occupancy states

Each sensor's output is assigned a confidence score and then passed into a decision-level fusion module. This module performs weighted voting, where the weight of each sensor can dynamically change based on context.

This fusion logic enhances reliability in real-world conditions like low lighting, partial obstruction, or high passenger density. Additionally, the architecture is scalable: new sensors (e.g., ultrasonic, capacitive touch) can be added to the pipeline with minimal code adjustment.

### System Strengths and Limitations

**Strengths:**

The hybrid AI-driven seat occupancy detection system presents a new combination of visual, thermal, and Wi-Fi CSI sensing to attain real-time seat availability monitoring in public transport. The multi-modal approach enhances accuracy and robustness in various lighting and occupancy conditions. For instance, where vision-based models like YOLOv5 may struggle in low-light or occluded environments, thermal sensors offer reliable heat signature detection. Similarly, Wi-Fi CSI data serves as a non-intrusive backup to detect human presence without the need for direct line-of-sight.

One of the major strengths of the system is its ability to operate locally on edge devices, minimizing latency and maintaining data privacy. Unlike cloud-based solutions, edge deployment ensures faster response time and avoids potential risks associated with centralized data processing. Furthermore, the modular nature of the system allows it to be adapted to different transport setups—ranging from city buses to metro coaches—without significant redesign.

**Limitations:**

Despite its promising features, there are limitations that need to be addressed:

- The initial hardware cost for thermal sensors and edge AI devices like Jetson Nano may not be feasible for large-scale deployments in low-budget transit systems
- Calibration of CSI signals and synchronization between sensors can present challenges in dynamic environments, especially in moving vehicles with signal noise

**Future Directions:**

Looking forward, the system can be further optimized with:

- Adaptive models that learn from real-time input
- Integration of GPS and scheduling APIs to enhance seat prediction accuracy
- Real-world pilot deployments and user interface development (mobile or dashboard) to validate usability and performance

Overall, the proposed system provides a strong foundation for future research in AI-based smart mobility and real-time occupancy analytics.

## Conclusion

This work introduces a real-time, AI-based hybrid seat occupancy detection system that harnesses the synergy between computer vision, Wi-Fi CSI sensing, and thermal imaging to enhance public transportation monitoring accuracy and responsiveness. By combining these sensor modalities with edge AI processing, the system overcomes core issues of data latency, privacy, and inaccurate detection in low-light or occluded conditions.

Our approach has much potential to enhance resource allocation, decrease overcrowding, and improve the transit experience through real-time visibility into available seats. Both commuters and transport authorities can gain from this by improving scheduling, route optimization, and fleet management. Additionally, the application of non-invasive sensors and local data processing makes it easy to comply with contemporary data protection regulations, and the system can be adapted to different geographic and legal environments.

While the simulations and architecture show promising performance metrics, the system is currently validated through controlled evaluations. Future implementations must include deployment in real-world public transit environments to evaluate robustness, adaptability, and scalability under dynamic conditions. Integration with smart transport dashboards, ticketing systems, and mobile apps will further strengthen its practical applicability.
